Real-time dynamic fluid surface simulation

Paul Legler, Victor Wang, Alex Yang

Current Progress

Though our progress wasn’t as steady and incremental as we had planned, as of today, we have been able to meet most of the goals we set out for both deliverables. During our work meetings, we researched different WebGL libraries and examples of simulations that were similar to the one we had in mind, and we combined the knowledge we got from each of these. As a result, some of our original plans have changed. One big change that we decided to make was to do away with modeling our pond with water particles in favor of modeling it with a height map. We felt that this was a much-needed simplification that would help us make quicker progress, as it cut out the learning of the particle physics as part of our to-do list. Overall, we found that much of our work involved looking at previous work and understanding it rather than implementing things from scratch.

Our current working project is heavily based off an example three.js water simulation. In it, when the user clicks down on the water, a wave starts from the point of contact in all directions. We detect when the user clicks, record this location, and pass the location into the height map shader. If multiple clicks occur, the waves combine in the expected manner. As the wave moves farther away from the origin of the click, multiplying by the viscosity decreases the magnitude of the wave.

To add a texture to the bottom of the tank, we added another THREE object (a PlaneBufferGeometry) below the plane of the water and rotated it accordingly. We used a texture we found online to mimic the sandy bottom of a pond. To make the water plane transparent, we adjusted a number of variables built in the Blinn-Phong shading model: opacity and transparency.
